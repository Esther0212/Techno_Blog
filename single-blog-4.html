Revenue Models for Social and Non-Social Enterprises: Insights from Mr. Karl Dominic Fajardo

  • By Esther Beuthel Eblacas
  • November 14, 2024

The Role of Revenue Models in Shaping Organizational Values

Reflecting on Mr. Karl Dominic Fajardo’s insights, it becomes evident that the design of revenue models is a direct reflection of an organization’s values and purpose. The way organizations approach revenue can differ drastically, especially between social and non-social enterprises, depending on whether their primary objective is societal impact or profit.

Revenue Models in Social Enterprises

In social enterprises, the revenue model is not just about financial strategy; it is closely tied to the organization’s mission. For example, the Fee-for-Service Model enables social enterprises to cover their operational costs while staying aligned with their purpose. This model charges clients directly for beneficial services, making every transaction an opportunity to contribute to social change. By connecting social impact with financial sustainability, it creates a cycle where profit becomes the means to fuel the mission.

Another important revenue model in social enterprises is the Cross-Subsidization Model. This model charges higher rates to wealthier clients in order to offer affordable services to underserved communities. It is an example of how social enterprises balance profitability with inclusivity, bridging gaps in access and equity. This approach underscores that profit is a means to achieve societal goals, not an end in itself.

Revenue Models in Non-Social Enterprises

Non-social enterprises, on the other hand, focus primarily on profit generation and shareholder value. Their revenue models are structured by boosting income and market growth. The Subscription-Based Model is a classic example of this approach. It involves offering a service for a recurring fee, which generates predictable, stable income streams. This model fosters customer loyalty and ensures long-term financial stability, with the focus on scalability and profitability.

Another common model in non-social enterprises is the Freemium Model, which provides a basic service for free while offering premium features at a cost. This model aims to attract a large user base, hoping that a percentage of them will eventually convert into paying customers. While the Freemium Model provides accessibility, its primary purpose is to maximize reach and increase revenue potential, rather than achieving societal impact.

Insights from Mr. Fajardo’s Reflections

In his reflection, Mr. Fajardo shared a personal thought:

“When I was younger, I wanted to start a business, and akong focus kay puro tabang. I will make the world a better place. I will make everyone happy. Gusto ko naa ta sa paradise.”

This quote reflects the initial idealism many entrepreneurs have, believing their business will bring about positive change. However, he later recognized that such dreams cannot be fulfilled 'without revenue,' and he lost more than 3 million in money.

From this, I learned an important lesson: while passion for societal good is essential, a business must have a sustainable revenue model to truly thrive and create lasting impact. Without revenue, no matter how noble the goal, an enterprise cannot function or achieve its mission. This insight encourages entrepreneurs to think about how revenue and purpose can be harmonized, ensuring both social impact and financial sustainability.
