Revenue Models for Social and Non-Social Enterprises: Insights from Mr. Karl Dominic Fajardo

  • By Esther Beuthel Eblacas
  • November 14, 2024

The Role of Revenue Models in Shaping Organizational Values

Reflecting on Mr. Karl Dominic Fajardo’s insights, it becomes evident that the design of revenue models is a direct reflection of an organization’s values and purpose. The way organizations approach revenue can differ drastically, especially between social and non-social enterprises, depending on whether their primary objective is societal impact or profit.

Revenue Models in Social Enterprises

In social enterprises, the revenue model is not just about financial strategy; it is closely tied to the organization’s mission. Social enterprises focus on addressing societal challenges while maintaining financial sustainability. Their revenue strategies emphasize inclusivity and impact:

  • Donation-Based: Funds raised through contributions from individuals or organizations.
  • Grants: Project-specific funding from governments or NGOs.
  • Fee-for-Service: Income earned by providing services like training or workshops.
  • Membership Subscriptions: Ongoing financial support from dedicated members.
  • Social Licensing: Partnerships with businesses to market socially responsible products or services.

Revenue Models in Non-Social Enterprises

Non-social enterprises, on the other hand, focus primarily on profit generation and shareholder value. Their revenue models are structured by boosting income and market growth.

  • Direct Sales: Selling goods or services directly to consumers.
  • Subscriptions: Offering recurring payment plans for access to products or services.
  • Freemium: Free basic features with an option to pay for advanced services.
  • Advertising Revenue: Monetizing platforms through ad placements.
  • Affiliate Marketing: Earning commissions by promoting third-party products.

Hybrid Revenue Models

Certain businesses adopt a blend of social and non-social revenue models, developing hybrid strategies to suit their unique goals. This method enables startups to leverage diverse opportunities while staying flexible to meet market needs.

Insights from Mr. Fajardo’s Reflections

In his reflection, Mr. Fajardo shared a personal thought:

“When I was younger, I wanted to start a business, and akong focus kay puro tabang. I will make the world a better place. I will make everyone happy. Gusto ko naa ta sa paradise.”

This quote reflects the initial idealism many entrepreneurs have, believing their business will bring about positive change. However, he later recognized that such dreams cannot be fulfilled 'without revenue,' and he lost more than 3 million in money.

From this, I learned an important lesson: while passion for societal good is essential, a business must have a sustainable revenue model to truly thrive and create lasting impact. Without revenue, no matter how noble the goal, an enterprise cannot function or achieve its mission. This insight encourages entrepreneurs to think about how revenue and purpose can be harmonized, ensuring both social impact and financial sustainability.
